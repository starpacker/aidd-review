# The AI Drug Revolution Needs a Revolution — Jacobson, 2025

**Full citation**: Jacobson RD. "The AI drug revolution needs a revolution." npj Drug Discovery. 2025;2:10. DOI: 10.1038/s44386-025-00013-6.
**Author**: Rachel DeVay Jacobson, co-founder and CSO of Powerhouse Biology
**Section relevance**: Section 1 (introduction), Section 7 (AI attribution), Section 8 (future directions)

## Key Findings

### Core Thesis
- AI drug discovery has potential BUT "not if it is limited to drug and target discovery in a human-agnostic manner"
- Scientists should "leverage AI to measure human responses to therapeutic treatments during the preclinical stages of drug development"
- This requires "incorporating functional human data, including its inherent variability, into preclinical research and validation studies while maintaining scientific reasoning throughout AI model training and analysis"

### Argument Structure
1. AI entered drug development with hope: "When the AI revolution marched into the drug development sector and flipped biotechs into techbios, it came with the hope that therapeutic programme failure rates would fall and the holy grails of cancer, dementia and even ageing would finally be found"
2. Efficiency gains exist, but clinical outcomes unchanged: "While cutting time (and costs) is commendable, we have yet to see these accelerated timelines translate into revolutionary improvements in clinical efficacy"
3. Current AI drugs target known targets with known mechanisms: "current AI-first molecules target previously established targets with mechanisms comparable to traditional drugs"
4. Canary in the coal mine warning: "if these first few AI drugs serve as proverbial canaries in the coal mine, our current AI approaches may not do much to improve clinical efficacy"
5. Solution: integrate human-relevant functional data into AI training

### "Human-Agnostic" Concept
- Current AI approaches focus on molecular properties (binding affinity, ADMET) without incorporating human physiological complexity
- The term implies AI trained on non-human or reductionist data cannot predict human clinical outcomes

## Our Take
- **Perfectly aligned with our review's thesis**: the gap between computational efficiency and clinical efficacy
- Her "human-agnostic" framing is powerful — we can cite and extend it
- The "techbios" vs "biotechs" distinction is useful framing
- Her argument complements our cascading failure analysis: even if AI finds the right target and right molecule, if the preclinical validation uses non-human-relevant models, clinical failure is inevitable
- We should extend her argument: the automation gap includes not just human data, but also the integration of synthesis, biological testing, and clinical feedback
- **Key for Section 7**: even AI-discovered drugs that reach Phase III (zasocitinib, baricitinib) used known targets and known mechanisms — AI accelerated but didn't innovate
