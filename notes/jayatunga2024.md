# AI Drugs in Clinical Trials — Jayatunga et al., 2024

**Full citation**: Jayatunga MKP, Ayers M, Bruens L, Jayanth D, Meier C. "How successful are AI-discovered drugs in clinical trials? A first analysis and emerging lessons." *Drug Discovery Today*. 2024 Jun;29(6):104009. DOI: 10.1016/j.drudis.2024.104009. PMID: 38692505.
**Authors**: All from Boston Consulting Group (BCG) offices in London, Amsterdam, and New Jersey.
**Section relevance**: Sections 1, 3, 4.1 (core evidence for precision paradox)

## Key Findings
- First systematic analysis of AI-discovered drugs in clinical trials
- Analyzed clinical pipelines of **39 AI-native biotech companies**
- By 2023, **75 molecules had entered clinical trials**, with **67 still in progress**
- **Phase I success rate: 80-90%** (vs. historical 40-65%)
  - Specific: **21/24 drugs met Phase I endpoints** (87.5% success rate)
  - This suggests AI is "highly capable of designing or identifying molecules with drug-like properties"
- **Phase II success rate: ~40%** (on a "limited sample size"), comparable to historic industry averages
  - Phase II N is NOT explicitly stated in available sources — described only as "limited sample size"
  - The ~40% figure matches traditional industry Phase II success of ~28-40%
- Core implication: AI excels at molecular optimization (Phase I = drug-likeness) but does NOT yet improve target validation or disease biology understanding (Phase II = efficacy)

## Methods
- Systematic identification of "AI-native Biotech companies" and their clinical pipelines
- Cross-referenced with clinical trial databases
- Analyzed success rates by phase vs. historic industry benchmarks
- **"AI-native" definition**: Companies that use AI as a core/foundational part of their drug discovery process (exact criteria from full text not publicly available, but distinguished from "AI-assisted" big pharma programs)
- Companies analyzed include entities like Insilico Medicine, Recursion, Exscientia (merged 2025), and others — full list in supplementary materials

## Limitations
- Limited Phase III data available at time of analysis (almost none)
- **Phase II sample size explicitly acknowledged as "limited"** — exact N not publicly disclosed, likely <20 drugs
- Definition of "AI-native" is inherently subjective — companies may overstate AI involvement
- **Key critique (from Elder, European Pharmaceutical Review)**: "Such claims have come from the companies themselves. Until they can be independently verified, some caution is in order." Need "publication in the peer-reviewed literature and authentication by researchers unaffiliated with the companies involved."
- Retrospective, non-randomized analysis — selection bias possible
- BCG authors have consulting relationships with AI pharma companies (potential COI)
- No breakdown by therapeutic area or AI method type
- Phase I success may partly reflect easier-to-drug targets being selected first

## Quotes (from abstract)
- "In Phase I we find AI-discovered molecules have an 80-90% success rate, substantially higher than historic industry averages."
- "This suggests, we argue, that AI is highly capable of designing or identifying molecules with drug-like properties."
- "In Phase II the success rate is ~40%, albeit on a limited sample size, comparable to historic industry averages."
- "Our findings highlight early signs of the clinical potential of AI-discovered molecules."

## Our Take
- **THE key reference** for our core thesis — provides the quantitative foundation for the "precision paradox"
- They quantify the gap but do NOT explain WHY Phase II drops. That's our contribution
- Must cite prominently in Sections 1 and 4.1
- Their N=75 molecules / 39 companies provides the systematic framing we need
- The Phase I vs Phase II gap is THE data point: AI optimizes molecules (drug-likeness, PK) but doesn't solve target validation or disease biology
- **Critical caveat for our review**: BCG authorship + limited Phase II N means we should cite this as "indicative" not "definitive"
- Need to note: Phase I success may reflect cherry-picking of well-validated targets rather than AI superiority per se
