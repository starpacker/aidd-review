# Why 90% of Clinical Drug Development Fails — Sun et al., 2022

**Full citation**: Sun D, Gao W, Hu H, Zhou S. "Why 90% of clinical drug development fails and how to improve it?" *Acta Pharmaceutica Sinica B*. 2022 Jul;12(7):3049-3062. DOI: 10.1016/j.apsb.2022.02.002. PMID: 35865092. PMC: PMC9293739.
**Authors**: Department of Pharmaceutical Sciences, University of Michigan
**Section relevance**: Section 2 (clinical failure rates baseline), Section 4 (ADMET/toxicity cascade), Section 5 (STAR framework as proposed solution)

## Key Findings

### The 90% Failure Rate Breakdown
From analysis of clinical trial data spanning **2010-2017**:

| Cause of Failure | Percentage |
|---|---|
| **Lack of clinical efficacy** | **40-50%** |
| **Unmanageable toxicity** | **30%** |
| **Poor drug-like properties (ADMET)** | **10-15%** |
| **Lack of commercial needs / poor strategic planning** | **10%** |

- The 90% figure applies to candidates already in **Phase I trials** — excluding preclinical failures, making overall attrition even higher
- Direct quote: "Analyses of clinical trial data from 2010 to 2017 show four possible reasons attributed to the 90% clinical failures"

### Historical Improvement in ADMET
- "Drug development failure due to poor drug-like properties has been significantly improved in the past 20 years"
- The reduction in ADMET-related failures (from historically higher rates to 10-15%) reflects improved computational and experimental ADMET screening in the 2000s-2010s
- But this improvement has been offset by persistent efficacy and toxicity failures

### The STAR Classification Framework
Authors propose **Structure-Tissue exposure/selectivity-Activity Relationship (STAR)** as a new drug optimization paradigm:

| Class | Potency/Selectivity | Tissue Exposure/Selectivity | Dose Required | Predicted Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **I** | High | High | Low | Superior efficacy/safety, HIGH success rate |
| **II** | High | Low | High | Efficacy achievable but HIGH toxicity risk |
| **III** | Low/Adequate | High | Low | Manageable toxicity, often OVERLOOKED candidates |
| **IV** | Low | Low | High | Inadequate efficacy AND safety — TERMINATE early |

Key insight: Traditional SAR (structure-activity relationship) optimization focuses on potency/selectivity but **ignores tissue exposure/selectivity**, leading to Class II drugs that are potent but toxic at required doses.

### Drug Examples
- The paper uses case studies including:
  - EGFR inhibitors (varying tissue selectivity profiles)
  - BTK inhibitors
  - SERMs (selective estrogen receptor modulators)
- These illustrate how drugs with similar target potency can have very different clinical outcomes based on tissue exposure profiles

## Methods
- **Review/Perspective article** — NOT an original data analysis
- Synthesizes published clinical trial failure data (2010-2017 period)
- References FDA-approved drug case studies to illustrate the STAR framework
- Conceptual framework proposal rather than empirical validation
- Data sources: Published literature reviews on Phase I-III outcomes, FDA approval records

## Limitations
- **Authors explicitly acknowledge**: "The drug examples in each class (I-IV) are estimation based on their plasma PK and clinical dose/efficacy/toxicity without experimental data since STAR is not currently used as a drug optimization process"
- The STAR framework is **proposed/conceptual** — not yet validated prospectively
- The 40-50% / 30% / 10-15% / 10% breakdown is sourced from other analyses, not original data collection
- No granular Phase-by-Phase breakdown (Phase I vs II vs III failure causes)
- Limited discussion of target validation as a failure mechanism
- The framework assumes tissue-level PK data is available, which it often is not in early discovery

## Key Quotes
- "Analyses of clinical trial data from 2010 to 2017 show four possible reasons attributed to the 90% clinical failures of drug development"
- "Drug development failure due to poor drug-like properties has been significantly improved in the past 20 years"
- The STAR system addresses a gap where "tissue exposure/selectivity in disease vs. normal tissues" is overlooked in traditional SAR optimization

## Our Take
- **Essential baseline reference** — the 40-50% efficacy / 30% toxicity / 10-15% ADMET breakdown is the most-cited framework for clinical failure causes
- Directly relevant to our argument: AI currently excels at the ADMET component (already improved from 10-15% to even lower), but does NOT address the dominant efficacy (40-50%) and toxicity (30%) failures
- The STAR framework provides a constructive angle for our "what needs to change" section — AI should incorporate tissue-level PK/exposure modeling, not just molecular-level optimization
- The historical ADMET improvement story parallels our argument: computational tools solved the "easy" problem first, but the hard problems (efficacy, in vivo toxicity) remain
- Cite in Section 2 to establish baseline failure rates
- Cite in Section 4 to argue that AI optimization disproportionately targets the smallest failure category (ADMET 10-15%) while neglecting efficacy (40-50%) and toxicity (30%)
- The STAR concept connects to our organ-on-chip / physiological modeling discussion
- **Caveat**: This is a review/perspective, not original data — the failure percentages ultimately trace to other primary analyses (Harrison 2016, Hay 2014, etc.)
